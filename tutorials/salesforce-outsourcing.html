Salesforce Insource vs Outsource — The Decision That Shapes Your Entire Programme

Should your Salesforce capability be built internally, outsourced to an SI partner, or managed through a hybrid model? This decision affects delivery speed, cost, quality and long-term agility more than almost any other programme decision.

👤 Vishal Sharma ⏱ 20 min read 📅 2026 🎯 CTOs, Programme Directors, Tech Leaders

The hidden stakes: The insource vs outsource decision for Salesforce is made early, often under time pressure, and is extremely difficult to reverse. Organisations that get it wrong spend years either rebuilding internal capability they should have built from the start, or unwinding SI dependencies that have become organisational liabilities.

The SI Landscape in 2026

The Salesforce implementation partner ecosystem ranges from global SIs (Accenture, Deloitte, IBM, Capgemini) who can field hundreds of Salesforce-certified consultants globally, to boutique specialists with deep expertise in specific clouds or industries, to independent contractors with targeted skills. Each segment of this market has different characteristics, cost profiles and risk profiles for enterprise customers.

Global SIs offer delivery scale and geographic coverage but often deliver through teams where the experienced architects who sold the engagement are rapidly replaced by junior consultants managing to utilise rates. Boutique specialists offer deeper expertise but limited capacity and potential key-person risk. The choice between them — and the question of whether to build internal capability instead — is one of the most consequential decisions a Salesforce programme makes.

The Case for Building Internal Capability

The argument for internal Salesforce capability is compelling when you view it over a five to ten year horizon. Internal Salesforce architects and developers who understand your business domain, your data model, your integration landscape and your organisational culture are enormously more effective than external consultants who must rebuild this context with every engagement.

The economics also favour insourcing at scale. A senior Salesforce architect costs £80-120k in permanent salary. The equivalent SI resource costs £800-1,200 per day — which means the SI resource costs more than a permanent hire in 70-150 days. Organisations running continuous Salesforce programmes (which most mature Salesforce customers are) pay the equivalent of two or three permanent headcount per SI architect per year.

The Case for Strategic SI Partnership

The argument for SI partnership is strongest in specific scenarios: organisations implementing Salesforce for the first time who lack any internal Salesforce expertise; implementations with aggressive timelines that require temporary delivery capacity beyond what the permanent team can provide; complex cloud-specific implementations (Marketing Cloud, Commerce Cloud, Experience Cloud) where deep specialist expertise is genuinely required; and greenfield transformations where the speed of access to proven implementation patterns justifies the premium cost.

Strategic SI partnership differs from tactical outsourcing. Strategic partnership means the SI is accountable for outcomes and quality, not just resource days. It means the internal team is actively learning from the SI, building capability that will reduce SI dependency over time. It means the governance model gives the internal team genuine authority over architectural decisions, not rubber-stamp approval of SI recommendations.

The Hybrid Model — Usually the Right Answer

For most mature enterprise Salesforce customers, the right answer is a hybrid model with clear delineation of responsibilities:

  • Internal team owns — Salesforce architecture, data model, security model, integration architecture, quality standards, acceptance testing and production governance
  • SI partner provides — additional delivery capacity for large programme sprints, specialist cloud expertise, independent review and challenge, and new capability development

This model preserves institutional knowledge internally while accessing SI scale and expertise when needed. The critical discipline is maintaining the internal team's authority over architectural decisions — the moment an SI is allowed to make architecture decisions without meaningful internal challenge, the organisation has ceded control of its most valuable Salesforce asset.

How to Govern an SI Relationship

SI governance is the discipline that separates strategic partnerships from expensive dependencies. The minimum governance framework for a Salesforce SI engagement:

  • Outcome-based contracts — measure SIs on delivered outcomes (working, tested functionality that users adopt), not on resource days delivered
  • Internal architecture authority — every significant architecture decision requires sign-off from an internal Salesforce architect, not just programme management approval
  • Knowledge transfer obligations — contractual requirements for documentation, pair programming and internal skill development as a deliverable of the engagement
  • Code review and quality standards — internal standards for Apex code quality, test coverage and naming conventions, applied consistently to SI-delivered code
  • Regular commercial reviews — quarterly review of SI costs against delivered value, with explicit authority to reduce scope or change resourcing if value is not being delivered

Building Internal Capability Over Time

Organisations that start with an SI-heavy model and intend to build internal capability over time rarely do so without an explicit plan. The pressures of programme delivery always create reasons to extend SI engagement rather than invest in slower internal capability building.

An effective insourcing transition plan designates internal hires for every SI architect role from the start of the programme, explicitly pairs internal hires with SI counterparts for knowledge transfer, and has a documented milestone at which each SI role is replaced or significantly reduced. The plan is reviewed quarterly and is a standing agenda item in programme governance.
