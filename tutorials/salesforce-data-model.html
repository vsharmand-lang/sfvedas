Salesforce Data Model Design — The Decisions That Haunt Enterprises for a Decade

The Salesforce data model decisions made in the first three months of an implementation shape the platform for the next ten years. Most enterprises make at least one irreversible mistake. Here is how to make better decisions.

👤 Vishal Sharma ⏱ 27 min read 📅 2026 🎯 Solution Architects, Tech Leads, CTOs

The painful truth: Data model decisions in Salesforce are asymmetric — they are easy to make and extraordinarily difficult to reverse. A poorly designed custom object structure, an incorrect use of the Account hierarchy, or a misapplied relationship type can constrain an organisation's Salesforce capabilities for a decade. This tutorial is about making those decisions deliberately.

Standard Objects vs Custom Objects

Salesforce ships with a rich set of standard objects — Account, Contact, Lead, Opportunity, Case, Campaign and dozens more. These objects come with pre-built fields, relationships, page layouts, automation support, reporting capabilities, and AppExchange compatibility. They are the platform's lingua franca.

The temptation in every implementation is to create custom objects that exactly mirror the organisation's existing terminology and processes. Instead of Account, perhaps the business calls them "Clients" and wants a custom object. Instead of Opportunity, they call them "Bids" or "Proposals." The reasoning is intuitive — match the platform to the business, not the business to the platform.

This reasoning, while understandable, usually leads to worse outcomes. Every custom object you create instead of extending a standard object means losing AppExchange compatibility, losing standard reporting, losing native integrations with Marketing Cloud and Service Cloud, and creating a maintenance burden that grows with every release.

The principle: Extend standard objects aggressively. Create custom objects only for genuinely novel data entities that have no logical mapping to any standard object. When in doubt, use Account.

The Account Model — The Most Consequential Decision

How you model Accounts in Salesforce is the single most consequential data model decision in any implementation. The Account object sits at the centre of the Salesforce data model. Contacts, Opportunities, Cases, Activities and hundreds of other objects relate to Accounts. Getting the Account model wrong means getting everything wrong.

The key questions that must be answered before the first Account record is created:

  • What is an Account? A company? A division? A location? A billing entity? Different answers lead to fundamentally different models
  • Do you need an Account hierarchy? Salesforce supports parent-child Account relationships natively. For enterprise B2B organisations dealing with subsidiaries, divisions and group structures, this is essential — but it must be designed before data is loaded, not retrofitted
  • How do you handle Person Accounts? Person Accounts merge the Account and Contact objects into a single record for individual consumers. Enabling Person Accounts is irreversible. Organisations in B2C or mixed B2B/B2C contexts must decide before enabling
  • What is your deduplication strategy? How will you prevent, detect and resolve duplicate Accounts? Salesforce's native duplicate management is limited — enterprise requirements typically demand additional tooling

Relationship Types and When Each Is Right

Salesforce offers three primary relationship types between objects, and the choice between them has significant implications:

Lookup Relationships are loosely coupled — the child record can exist independently of the parent, and deletion of the parent does not cascade to children. Use lookups when the child entity has independent meaning, when the relationship might be nullable, or when you need to relate records across different ownership models.

Master-Detail Relationships are tightly coupled — the child record cannot exist without the parent, deletion of the parent cascades to all children, and the child's ownership and sharing is controlled by the master. Master-Detail enables Roll-Up Summary Fields — one of Salesforce's most powerful native aggregation features — which are not available on Lookup relationships. Use Master-Detail when the child record is genuinely subordinate to the parent and has no independent existence.

Many-to-Many Relationships are implemented in Salesforce through Junction Objects — a custom object with two Master-Detail relationships to the objects being related. This is the correct approach for genuinely many-to-many data relationships but introduces complexity that must be justified by the business requirement.

Converting a Lookup to a Master-Detail after data has been loaded is possible but operationally complex. Converting a Master-Detail to a Lookup loses Roll-Up Summary Fields. Make these decisions deliberately, not retrospectively.

Schema Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These are the data model patterns that experienced Salesforce architects see repeatedly in troubled implementations:

  • The Everything Object — a single custom object with hundreds of fields attempting to represent multiple different business entities. Usually created by organisations that resist building multiple objects. Results in complex page layouts, poor performance and impossible reporting
  • The Field Proliferation Problem — adding fields to standard objects without discipline. Every unnecessary field on Account, Contact or Opportunity adds to page load time, clutters the interface and makes reporting more complex. Every field should justify its existence
  • The Wrong Hierarchy Direction — relating objects in the wrong direction. For example, creating multiple Opportunity custom objects as children of a master Opportunity rather than using the standard line item model. This creates reporting complexity and breaks standard functionality
  • Ignoring External IDs — failing to designate External ID fields on key objects from the outset makes data integration and migration substantially harder. Every object that will receive data from external systems needs a properly indexed External ID
  • Over-using Text fields — storing data in free-text fields that should be Picklists, Numbers or Dates makes reporting, filtering and automation unreliable. Data type decisions matter

Data Volume and Performance Implications

Data model decisions made for a 50,000 record dataset behave very differently at 5 million records. Salesforce's multi-tenant architecture means that performance at scale is a shared concern — poorly designed queries and data models in one org affect platform performance for other customers, which is why the platform has SOQL governor limits.

Design your data model for 10x your current volume from day one. Specific considerations:

  • Indexing strategy — Salesforce automatically indexes standard fields and fields marked as External IDs. Custom fields used in SOQL WHERE clauses on high-volume objects should be considered for custom indexing through Salesforce support
  • Large Data Volume (LDV) implications — objects expected to exceed 1 million records require specific architectural considerations: skinny tables, custom indexes and careful query design
  • Roll-Up Summary Field performance — Roll-Up Summary Fields on Master-Detail relationships recalculate on every child record change. On high-volume objects with frequent updates, this can cause record lock contention. Design roll-up strategies carefully

The True Cost of Getting It Wrong

Data model remediation — fixing a fundamentally flawed Salesforce data model after the platform has been in production — is one of the most expensive and disruptive activities an organisation can undertake. The costs are both direct and indirect:

Direct costs include the consulting and internal time required to design the new model, migrate data from the old structure, update all automations, reports, dashboards and integrations that reference the old structure, retest the entire platform and retrain users.

Indirect costs include the operational disruption during migration, the productivity loss during the period when teams are using workarounds, the technical debt accumulated in integrations that have been built around the flawed model, and the opportunity cost of investment that could have been spent on new capability rather than remediation.

A data model remediation project on a mature Salesforce org typically costs 3-5x the original implementation investment. The organisations that avoid this cost are those that invest in architecture quality at the outset — engaging experienced Salesforce architects, conducting design reviews before build, and challenging requirements that lead to non-standard patterns.
