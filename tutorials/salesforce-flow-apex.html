Flow vs Apex — The Decision Framework Every Salesforce Architect Needs

'Should this be a Flow or Apex?' is asked dozens of times in every Salesforce implementation. Most teams answer it based on who is available rather than what is appropriate. A rigorous framework for making the right architectural choice every time.

👤 Vishal Sharma ⏱ 18 min read 📅 2026 🎯 Solution Architects, Tech Leads, Senior Developers

The false dichotomy: The Flow vs Apex debate is often framed as declarative versus programmatic, admin versus developer, fast versus maintainable. These framings obscure the actual decision criteria. The right framework evaluates complexity, maintainability, performance, testability and business ownership — not just who can build it faster today.

When Flow is the Right Choice

Salesforce's strategic direction is clear: Flow is the future of declarative automation on the platform. Workflow Rules and Process Builder are deprecated; Flow is their replacement and the platform's primary automation tool. This strategic alignment should bias architectural decisions towards Flow where it is capable.

Flow is the right choice when:

  • The business logic is straightforward enough to be maintained by a Salesforce administrator without developer involvement
  • The process is triggered by standard Salesforce events (record create, update, scheduled run, user action)
  • The data volumes involved are moderate and the logic does not require complex collection manipulation
  • The process needs to be modified frequently by business users in response to changing requirements
  • UI automation (screen flows, guided processes) is part of the requirement

When Apex is the Right Choice

Apex is essential — not merely preferable — in specific scenarios where Flow's capabilities are insufficient:

  • Complex collection manipulation — sorting, filtering, grouping and aggregating large collections of records requires programmatic logic that Flow cannot express efficiently or reliably
  • Bulkification requirements — processing thousands of records in a single transaction requires explicit bulkification patterns that are natural in Apex but difficult to guarantee in Flow
  • Complex integration logic — callouts with conditional logic, error handling, retry patterns and response parsing are better expressed in Apex
  • Reusable business logic — logic that must be invoked from multiple contexts (triggers, APIs, batch jobs, other Apex classes) belongs in an Apex service layer, not duplicated in multiple flows
  • Custom exception handling — sophisticated error handling with specific recovery patterns requires Apex

Performance — The Most Misunderstood Dimension

Performance is the most frequently cited reason for choosing Apex over Flow, and also the most frequently misunderstood. The reality:

Flow performance is acceptable for most use cases. For single-record operations triggered by user actions, the performance difference between Flow and equivalent Apex is not perceptible to users. Choosing Apex purely for performance on single-record processes is premature optimisation.

Flow performance degrades significantly in bulk contexts. When a Flow is triggered by a data load of 10,000 records, each record potentially triggering its own Flow execution with its own SOQL queries, the governor limit and performance implications are severe. Apex with explicit bulkification handles this scenario correctly; Flow typically does not without careful architectural design.

The performance test: Before choosing Flow for any automation that might be triggered in bulk, simulate a bulk data operation in a sandbox and measure governor limit consumption. If the result approaches limits, Apex bulkification is required.

Testability and Quality

For many years, testability strongly favoured Apex — unit testing of Apex classes is well-supported, while Flow testing was limited to manual execution. This gap has narrowed significantly with Salesforce's investment in Flow testing capabilities.

Flow test coverage is now measurable and reportable. Salesforce has introduced Flow testing within the platform, and tools like the Salesforce CLI's test runner can execute Flow tests as part of a CI/CD pipeline. Screen Flows can be tested with Jest-based component tests. The testing gap that historically justified Apex for quality-sensitive processes is smaller than it was two years ago.

However, Apex unit testing remains more mature, more flexible and more integrated into professional CI/CD pipelines than Flow testing. For business-critical, high-risk automation, Apex's testability advantage remains meaningful.

Long-Term Maintainability

The maintainability question is nuanced and depends heavily on who will maintain the automation over its lifetime:

Flow maintenance by business users: A well-structured Flow can be maintained by a skilled Salesforce administrator without developer involvement. For automation that changes frequently in response to business rule changes, this administrator-maintainability is genuinely valuable and reduces the cost of ongoing change.

Complex Flow maintainability: A Flow with 50+ elements, multiple decision branches and nested sub-flows is not significantly easier to maintain than equivalent Apex. The visual interface that makes simple Flows accessible becomes overwhelming at high complexity. Very complex Flows should be refactored into Apex — the visual metaphor does not scale.

Apex service layer maintainability: Well-structured Apex with a clear service layer pattern, comprehensive tests and good documentation is highly maintainable by experienced developers. It is not maintainable by administrators. This is not a problem if the team structure accounts for it.

The Hybrid Pattern

The pattern that delivers the best of both worlds for most complex scenarios is the hybrid: a Flow that handles the user experience and basic orchestration, calling Apex actions (Invocable Methods) for the complex logic that benefits from programmatic implementation.

This pattern gives you:

  • Flow's declarative UI and approachability for the process structure that changes frequently
  • Apex's programmatic power for the logic that requires it
  • Clear separation of concerns — business process orchestration in Flow, business logic in Apex
  • The ability for administrators to modify the process flow while developers maintain the underlying logic

Invocable Methods — Apex methods annotated with @InvocableMethod — are the bridge between Flow and Apex. Design your Invocable Methods to be genuinely bulkified (accepting List parameters, not single records) from the outset, because Flows can call them in bulk contexts.

Key Takeaways

  • ✦ Flow is the correct first choice for most business process automation.
  • ✦ Apex is essential when performance at scale, complex logic or reusability is required.
  • ✦ Bulkification is critical — Flows that call Apex or perform DML in loops fail at scale.
  • ✦ Flow testability has improved significantly — use it.
  • ✦ The hybrid pattern (Flow calling Apex actions) combines the strengths of both.
