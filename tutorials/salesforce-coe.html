Building a Salesforce Centre of Excellence — Why Most Fail and What the Successful Ones Do Differently

A Salesforce Centre of Excellence is supposed to be the organisational structure that ensures consistent, high-quality Salesforce delivery across an enterprise. Most never fulfil that potential. A deep look at what actually makes them work.

👤 Vishal Sharma ⏱ 21 min read 📅 2026 🎯 CTOs, Programme Directors, Tech Leaders

The CoE paradox: Salesforce Centres of Excellence are established to reduce duplication, increase quality and accelerate delivery. Many end up creating bureaucracy, slowing delivery and being dissolved within three years. The difference between a CoE that adds value and one that adds overhead is not the intention — it is the design.

Why Organisations Create CoEs

The Salesforce Centre of Excellence concept emerges from a common pattern in large organisations: multiple business units independently implement Salesforce, each making different architectural decisions, using different standards, building different capabilities and creating integration complexity that compounds over time. The CoE is the organisational response — a central function to provide architectural governance, shared services and standards across all Salesforce implementations.

The theoretical benefits are clear: consistent architecture reduces integration costs; shared capability reduces duplication; centralised expertise raises quality; governance prevents the expensive mistakes that independent teams make. When these benefits are realised, the CoE delivers genuine value. When the CoE becomes primarily a governance bureaucracy, it delivers primarily overhead.

The Most Common CoE Failure Modes

CoEs fail in recognisable patterns:

  • The Gatekeeper CoE — every architectural decision, every deployment, every new feature requires CoE approval. Teams spend more time navigating the approval process than building. The CoE becomes the bottleneck that slows the entire Salesforce programme
  • The Ivory Tower CoE — CoE architects design standards in isolation from delivery teams, producing guidelines that are theoretically sound but practically unworkable. Delivery teams ignore the standards; the CoE becomes irrelevant
  • The Understaffed CoE — established with ambition but funded with one or two people who cannot possibly provide the governance, enablement and architecture support the model requires. Inevitably fails to deliver on its mandate
  • The Tool-Only CoE — invests in tooling and processes without investing in the people and relationships that make a CoE effective. The tools are used incorrectly; the processes are not followed; the CoE has no influence

CoE Operating Models — Centralised vs Federated

Two primary operating models for Salesforce CoEs, each appropriate for different organisational contexts:

Centralised CoE — all Salesforce capability (architecture, development, administration) resides in a central function that serves all business units. Best for organisations with a single integrated operating model, shared customer data and a culture of central IT. Creates clear accountability and consistent standards but can create distance from business unit needs and delivery bottlenecks at scale.

Federated CoE — Salesforce capability is distributed in business units, with the CoE providing architecture standards, shared tooling, training and governance rather than direct delivery. Best for large, autonomous organisations with diverse Salesforce use cases. Enables business unit agility within consistent standards but requires strong governance to prevent fragmentation.

Most enterprise organisations are better served by a federated model that provides standards and enablement rather than a centralised model that provides delivery. The centralised model only works when the CoE is large enough to actually serve the delivery needs of the organisation.

What a CoE Should Actually Own

The most effective CoEs own a specific, bounded scope:

  • Architecture standards — the documented principles and patterns that all Salesforce implementations must follow: data model standards, integration patterns, security baselines, naming conventions
  • Shared platform services — capabilities that every Salesforce team needs and that are most efficiently centralised: SSO configuration, Connected App governance, MuleSoft shared APIs, Salesforce licence management
  • Architecture review — lightweight review of significant design decisions against standards. Not approval of every decision — review of significant ones
  • Community of practice — the forum where Salesforce practitioners across the organisation share knowledge, problems and solutions. Often the highest-value CoE activity for the lowest cost
  • Talent development — certification support, training programmes, learning paths and career frameworks for Salesforce professionals across the organisation

Measuring CoE Effectiveness

The most common CoE measurement mistake is measuring CoE activity rather than CoE impact. Activity metrics — standards documents produced, architecture reviews conducted, training sessions delivered — measure what the CoE does, not what it achieves.

Impact metrics measure what the delivery teams achieve as a result of CoE support:

  • Delivery team velocity — are delivery teams shipping faster and with more confidence than before the CoE?
  • Production incident rate — has the frequency of production incidents attributable to architectural problems reduced?
  • Standards adoption rate — what percentage of Salesforce implementations follow the CoE's architectural standards?
  • Time-to-architecture-decision — how long does it take delivery teams to get architectural guidance from the CoE? If this is measured in weeks, the CoE is a bottleneck

Building a CoE That Lasts

The CoEs that endure share these design characteristics:

  • Start small, prove value, expand. The biggest CoE failure is scaling before the model is proven. Start with standards and community of practice — the lowest cost, highest legitimacy activities. Expand when you can demonstrate that the CoE makes delivery teams better.
  • Hire the right first architect. The CoE's first senior architect is its most important hire — someone who combines deep Salesforce platform expertise with the communication skills and organisational credibility to influence delivery teams without authority
  • Be an enabler, not a gatekeeper. Every interaction with a delivery team should leave them more capable than before. Every process should be designed to reduce friction, not add it
  • Review and refresh standards regularly. A standard that was right in 2022 may be wrong in 2026 as the platform has evolved. CoEs that maintain static standards for years become obstacles rather than guides
